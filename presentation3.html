Generational Economics Question 1

Question 1: The "golden rule" is the level at which steady-state consumption is at a maximum, given the parameters of the model. Steady state consumption is?

Answer question 1

The "golden rule" is the level at which steady-state consumption is at a maximum, given the parameters of the model. Steady state consumption is

\[c^* = (1-s^*)\cdot f[k^*(s^*)] = f[k^*(s^*)] - s^*f[k^*(s^*)]\] Where, \[ 0<s^*<1\] We also have that, at the steady state where capital is constant \[s^*f[k^*(s^*)] = \delta k^*(s^*)\]

By substituting the optimal saving rate into the optimal consumption rate we get, \[\frac {\partial c^*}{\partial s^*} = f'[k^*(s^*)]\cdot \frac {{\rm d}k^*}{{\rm d}s^*} - \delta \frac {{\rm d}k^*}{{\rm d}s^*} =0\] \[ f'[k^*(s^*)]\ - \delta \big)\frac {{\rm d}k^*}{{\rm d}s^*} =0 \implies f'[k^*(s^*)] = \delta\]

Environmental Economics

-We consider 2 emission sources \(M_1\) and \(M_2\) along with three receptors \(Q_1,Q_2,Q_3\) where the emissions are deposited and damage is generated. We also include \(a_{ji}\), the fraction of emssion transfered from source \(i\) to receptor \(j\). This is represented in vector matrix notation as \(Q=AM\).

Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} a_{11}, a_{12} \\[0.3em] a_{21},a_{22} \\[0.3em] a_{31},a_{32} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \sqrt0.08, \sqrt0.08 \\[0.3em] 0.1,0.2 \\[0.3em] \sqrt0.285,0 \end{bmatrix}\). Let \(B(M_i) = 6M_i-0.5M_{i}^2\),\(D(Q_j)=0.5Q_{j}^2\) be Benefits of emissions and damage of depositions. Total damage is given by \(TD = \sum_{j=1}^{3} D(Q_j)\).
Marginal benefits of \(M_1\): \(MB(M_1)=B'(M_1)=6-M_1\) Total Marginal damage of emissions \(M_1\):

\begin{equation} \begin{split} TD & =\sum_{j=1}^{3} 0.5Q_{j}^2 \\ & = \sum_{j=1}^{3} 0.5(a_{j1}M_1 + a{j2}M_2)^2 \\ &= 0.5(\sqrt0.08M_1 + \sqrt0.08M_2)^2 + 0.5(0.1M_1 + 0.2M_2)^2 + 0.5(\sqrt0.285M_1)^2 \end{split} \end{equation}

We take the partial derivative of \(D(Q_j)\) for \(j = 1,2,3\) with repect to \(M_1\) by the application of the chain rule: \'

\begin{equation} \begin{split} & \frac{\partial D(Q_1)}{\partial M_1} = 0.08M_1 + 0.08M_2 \\& \frac{\partial D(Q_2)}{\partial M_1} = 0.01M_1 + 0.02M_2 \\& \frac{\partial D(Q_3)}{\partial M_1} = 0.285M_1 \end{split} \end{equation}
The final Answer
Total marginal damage is simply the sum of the above, so we have total marginal damage of \(M_1\): \(0.375M_1 + 0.1M_2\).
